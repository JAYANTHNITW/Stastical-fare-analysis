01 — Overview

Problem, objective & research question

Problem statement

In the fast paced taxi booking sector, making the most of revenue is essential for long term success and driver happiness. The goal is to use data-driven insights to maximize revenue streams for taxi drivers, focusing on the relationship between payment type and fare amount.

Objective

Run an A/B test to examine the relationship between total fare and method of payment, using hypothesis testing and descriptive statistics to find out whether there is a meaningful difference in fares between credit card and cash payments.

Research question

Is there a relationship between total fare amount and payment type — and can customers be nudged toward payment methods that generate higher revenue for drivers, without negatively impacting the customer experience?

02 — Data & Cleaning

From 6.4M raw trips to an analysis-ready set

Source data is the NYC yellow taxi trip records for January 2020 (18 raw columns). The pipeline below mirrors the cleaning steps applied before any statistical testing.

1
Datetime conversion

Pickup and dropoff timestamps converted to proper datetime objects to enable time-based calculations.

2
Trip duration

Duration derived in minutes from the pickup/dropoff timestamp difference.

3
Column selection

Narrowed to the five columns relevant to this question: passenger count, payment type, fare amount, trip distance, duration.

4
Missing & duplicate records

65,441 rows (~1.02% of the dataset) had missing passenger_count / payment_type values and were dropped; duplicate rows were removed.

5
Scope restriction

Kept only card & cash payment types (excluding the smaller no-charge / dispute / voided categories) and passenger counts between 1 and 5.

6
Invalid value & outlier check

Non-positive fare, distance, and duration values were filtered out; an IQR-based outlier check was run across fare_amount, trip_distance, and duration.

Final analysis set: 2,780,283 trips across 5 columns — roughly 43% of the original month of data survives the cleaning pipeline.

04 — Hypothesis Testing

Is the fare gap statistically significant?

A two-sample independent Welch's t-test (unequal variance) was run comparing fare_amount for card vs. cash trips.

06 — Conclusion

What this means for drivers

Card payments carry a statistically significant fare premium over cash — averaging $13.11 vs. $11.76, a gap confirmed by a Welch's t-test (t = 169.21, p < 0.001) that is far too large to be explained by chance across 2.78M trips. Card is also already the dominant method, used in 67.3% of trips.

Trip duration alone explains 76% of the variance in fare amount, confirming duration is a strong — though incomplete — predictor; distance, zone, traffic, and surge effects likely account for much of the remaining variance.

For the original research question — nudging customers toward higher-revenue payment methods without harming experience — the data supports encouraging card payments where feasible (e.g. easy in-app tipping, contactless readers), rather than any change to how a fare itself is calculated.
